# 1. Public Transit: The Route Network

Latchkey doesn’t build “infrastructure” the human way. It **discovers, reinforces, and maintains routes**.

### Core Principle
Transit is:
- **path-based, not vehicle-based**
- **memory-driven, not map-driven**
- **redundant by necessity**

---

## The Three-Layer Transit System

### 1. **The Wet Lines (Subsurface Highways)**
Storm drains, culverts, sewer-adjacent channels.

- Used for:
  - long-distance movement
  - bad weather travel
  - moving young safely
- Flow follows water, but raccoons maintain **dry ledges** and debris-stabilized crossings

**Raccoon-specific detail:**
They deliberately wedge sticks, trash, and fabric into cracks to create **micro-bridges** just above water level.

**Tradeoffs:**
- + Safe from predators and humans
- + Reliable navigation (water always leads somewhere)
- – Flood risk
- – Slower, single-file in narrow pipes

---

### 2. **The Shadow Routes (Surface Grid)**
Alleys, fence lines, backyard perimeters, park edges.

- Used for:
  - food runs
  - social encounters
  - flexible detours

These are the most dynamic routes. They change nightly based on:
- trash availability
- human activity patterns
- dog presence

**Key mechanism:**
Raccoons maintain routes through **scent + repetition**, not markings. A “street” exists because enough raccoons used it last night.

**Tradeoffs:**
- + Fast and direct
- + High opportunity (food, information)
- – Exposure to humans, cars, dogs

---

### 3. **The High Ways (Canopy & Roof Network)**
Trees, power lines, fences, rooftops.

- Used for:
  - rapid travel
  - scouting
  - elite movement (more skilled individuals)

**Raccoon-specific detail:**
Certain individuals specialize in these routes and effectively become **informal couriers**.

**Tradeoffs:**
- + Fastest travel
- + Lowest ground risk
- – Requires skill
- – Gaps can be dangerous

---

## Transit Hubs (This is where it gets city-like)

Latchkey has **convergence points**, not stations.

Examples:
- the base of a large oak connecting canopy to fence network
- a storm drain opening behind a restaurant
- a dumpster cluster with multiple escape vectors

At these hubs:
- traffic spikes at predictable hours
- information spreads
- conflicts happen

These are the closest thing to “downtown.”

---

## Scheduling (Yes, really)

Raccoons don’t run on clocks, but they **absolutely run on timing patterns**:

- Early night: outbound traffic (foraging)
- Midnight peak: maximum cross-city movement
- Pre-dawn: return flow

Some routes become:
- **“quiet corridors”** (safe but low traffic)
- **“rush paths”** (fast but competitive)

---

## Maintenance System

No central authority maintains transit.

Instead:
- routes persist if they are **useful**
- routes disappear if they are **dangerous or unproductive**

However, raccoons *do*:
- reinforce stable crossings
- remember hazards (dogs, traps, human interference)
- teach young preferred paths by repetition

This creates a **self-healing network**.

---

## Delightfully Specific Raccoon Choices

- A slightly ajar fence panel becomes a **major arterial route** within 48 hours.
- A reliable restaurant dumpster creates a **permanent transit hub**, even if it’s out of the way.
- A single aggressive dog can reroute an entire neighborhood’s traffic patterns.
- Juveniles treat transit like parkour and often discover new routes adults later adopt.

---

## Key Tradeoff (City-Level)

Latchkey’s transit is:
- incredibly adaptable
- impossible to centrally control

This means:
- the city is resilient
- but never fully optimized

There is always inefficiency, duplication, and risk.
And that’s exactly why it survives.

# 2. Trash & Sanitation: The Backbone System

Humans think raccoons “live in trash.”

That’s wrong.

**Latchkey runs on trash.**
It is a full-stack resource extraction, sorting, and redistribution system built on human waste streams.

---

## Core Principle

There is no “garbage.” Only:
- **food**
- **materials**
- **hazards**

Sanitation is not about cleanliness. It’s about:
- **keeping flows usable**
- **preventing spoilage and disease**
- **maintaining access to high-value waste**

---

## The Three-Stage System

### 1. **Acquisition (The Pull Phase)**

This is what most people think raccoons do, but in Latchkey it’s structured.

Primary sources:
- residential bins (predictable, high volume)
- restaurant dumpsters (high value, high competition)
- park trash cans (low yield, low risk)

**Raccoon-specific behavior:**
- Individuals memorize **trash schedules by block**
- Some specialize in **lid-opening techniques** for specific bin types
- Others specialize in **timing** (arriving minutes after humans put bins out)

There is real skill differentiation here.

---

### 2. **Sorting (The Quiet Work)**

This is the hidden layer that makes the system viable.

Sorting happens at:
- drainage edges
- flat rooftops
- under-bridge platforms
- quiet corners of parks

Materials are roughly classified into:

#### **A. Immediate Food**
- consumed on site
- highest competition

#### **B. Portable Food**
- carried to safer areas
- often cached

#### **C. Structural Materials**
- cardboard
- cloth
- plastic sheets
Used for:
- insulation
- bedding
- bridge-building (in Wet Lines)

#### **D. Containers**
- cups
- jars
- bags

These are *extremely valuable* because raccoons think in terms of **containment**.

#### **E. Hazards**
- sharp metal
- toxic residues
- sticky substances (very bad for paws)

Hazards are not removed globally, but they are **locally avoided and socially remembered**.

---

### 3. **Redistribution (The Flow Layer)**

Nothing stays where it’s found unless it’s safe.

- food moves toward **low-risk consumption zones**
- materials move toward **housing clusters**
- containers circulate widely and unpredictably

This creates a **constant micro-logistics system**:
- short-distance hauling
- repeated trips
- opportunistic redistribution

No one is “in charge,” but the system works because:
- raccoons remember good spots
- they revisit and rework them nightly

---

## Sanitation (In Their Terms)

Latchkey defines “clean” as:
- no rot buildup
- no blocked paths
- no concentrated hazard zones

### How that’s achieved:

#### 1. **Dispersal Over Accumulation**
Raccoons instinctively spread things out.

Why:
- reduces smell concentration (predator avoidance)
- prevents one area from becoming unusable

#### 2. **Water-Assisted Cleaning**
Food is often:
- dunked
- rinsed
- moved near water before eating

This has side effects:
- reduces contamination
- keeps feeding areas usable longer

#### 3. **Abandonment Protocol**
If a site becomes:
- too foul
- too risky
- too depleted

…it is simply abandoned.

This is critical:
Latchkey **does not rehabilitate sites**.
It **rotates away from them**.

---

## Economic Implications

This system creates real structure:

### High-Value Zones
- restaurant districts
- event venues
- grocery store dumpsters

These areas:
- attract higher density
- produce more conflict
- create semi-stable “territories”

### Low-Value Zones
- residential areas with sealed bins
- overly clean neighborhoods

These become:
- transit corridors
- fallback zones

---

## Specialization (This is where it gets fun)

You start to see roles emerge:

- **Crackers**
  Experts at opening containers

- **Timers**
  Know exactly when trash appears

- **Carriers**
  Strong individuals who move bulk materials

- **Scouts**
  Identify new waste sources (construction sites, events, etc.)

None of these are formal jobs.
But the behaviors cluster.

---

## Delightfully Specific Raccoon Choices

- A raccoon will drag an entire slice of pizza 200 feet to eat it in a *slightly safer shadow*.
- Yogurt cups become prized not for food, but for their **perfect paw-sized containment geometry**.
- A reliable bakery dumpster can shift the entire neighborhood’s nightly migration pattern.
- One spilled bag of flour can temporarily break a zone because nobody wants sticky paws.

---

## System-Level Tradeoff

Latchkey’s sanitation model is:
- extremely efficient at **resource extraction**
- surprisingly good at **preventing localized collapse**

But:
- it cannot eliminate hazards globally
- it depends entirely on **continuous human waste production**

This means the city is:
- **resilient in the short term**
- **structurally dependent in the long term**

---

At this point, we’ve got:
- movement (transit)
- resource flow (trash system)

- density

# 3. Housing Typologies: Where Raccoons Actually Live

## Core Principle

Housing is optimized for:
- **concealment first**
- **multiple exits always**
- **thermal stability**
- **proximity to routes, not views**

No one is picking a place because it’s “nice.”
They’re picking it because they can:
- disappear quickly
- sleep safely
- leave without being seen

---

## The Five Primary Housing Types

Each one creates a different kind of neighborhood.

---

## 1. **Den Clusters (Tree & Root Systems)**
*“The Old Neighborhoods”*

These are:
- hollow trees
- root cavities
- dense ivy-covered trunks

Often found in:
- parks
- riverbanks
- older residential areas

### Structure
- multiple dens within a small radius
- loosely shared territory
- stable over multiple seasons

### Social Pattern
- higher tolerance for proximity
- informal “neighbor awareness”
- mothers with kits strongly prefer these

### Tradeoffs
- + excellent concealment
- + temperature regulation
- + low human disturbance
- – limited expansion
- – dependent on aging trees

### Raccoon-specific choice
Raccoons will **reuse the same den lineages for years**, passing knowledge of “good trees” across generations.

This is as close as Latchkey gets to *heritage housing*.

---

## 2. **Structural Voids (Buildings & Human Infrastructure)**
*“The Dense Urban Core”*

These are:
- attics
- crawlspaces
- wall cavities
- abandoned structures

### Structure
- highly insulated
- often complex internal pathways
- connected to multiple entry points

### Social Pattern
- higher density
- more conflict
- more transient occupants

### Tradeoffs
- + warmth (huge advantage in winter)
- + protection from weather
- + proximity to high-value trash zones
- – human interference risk
- – sudden eviction (repairs, pest control)

### Raccoon-specific choice
They memorize **micro-entry points**:
- loose vents
- warped siding
- gaps under eaves

A single overlooked opening can support an entire micro-community.

---

## 3. **Drain Dwellings (Subsurface Living)**
*“The Infrastructure Class”*

These are:
- widened sections of storm drains
- dry culvert shelves
- pipe intersections

### Structure
- linear, tunnel-based living
- often organized along transit lines

### Social Pattern
- lower density per node
- highly mobile residents
- strong familiarity with Wet Lines

### Tradeoffs
- + excellent connectivity
- + low visibility
- + stable during normal weather
- – catastrophic during flooding
- – limited comfort

### Raccoon-specific choice
Residents maintain **elevated bedding platforms** out of debris to stay above sudden water flow.

It’s crude, but effective.

---

## 4. **Rooftop & Canopy Nests**
*“The Edge Specialists”*

These are:
- dense tree forks
- roof corners
- intersections of structures and foliage

### Structure
- small, often temporary
- built from scavenged soft materials

### Social Pattern
- more solitary individuals
- younger or more agile raccoons
- short-term stays

### Tradeoffs
- + fast escape routes
- + access to High Ways
- – exposure to weather
- – less secure

### Raccoon-specific choice
These raccoons prioritize **mobility over comfort**.
They are often the first to discover new routes or food sources.

---

## 5. **Hybrid Sites (The Premium Real Estate)**
*“Where Everything Connects”*

These are rare and highly valued:
- a tree adjacent to a building with a crawlspace
- a storm drain opening beneath a restaurant deck
- a park edge touching a residential trash corridor

### Structure
- access to multiple layers (surface, subsurface, canopy)
- multiple escape routes in different directions

### Social Pattern
- contested but stable once “claimed”
- often occupied by experienced individuals

### Tradeoffs
- + unmatched access
- + flexibility in danger
- – constant pressure from others trying to move in

### Raccoon-specific choice
These sites are less about dominance and more about **competence**.
If you can’t manage the complexity, you lose it.

---

## Neighborhood Formation

Latchkey doesn’t have districts. It has **overlapping housing ecosystems**.

A typical “neighborhood” might include:
- a cluster of tree dens (families)
- a nearby attic population (dense, competitive)
- a drain corridor (transit-linked residents)

These groups:
- share routes
- compete for food
- recognize each other over time

---

## Housing Lifecycle

Housing is not permanent.

A site can:
- **emerge quickly** (new opening, fallen tree)
- **become prime** (discovered + reinforced)
- **collapse suddenly** (human repair, flooding, tree removal)

So raccoons:
- maintain **backup sites**
- regularly “test” alternate dens
- never fully commit to a single location

---

## Delightfully Specific Raccoon Choices

- A raccoon will choose a slightly colder den if it has **two exits instead of one**.
- Soft materials (dryer lint, insulation, fabric scraps) are treated as **luxury upgrades**.
- A den with a **direct drop to a fence line** is considered elite because of escape speed.
- Some raccoons maintain **decoy sleeping spots** they rarely use, just in case.

---

## System-Level Tradeoff

Latchkey housing is:
- incredibly flexible
- highly resilient to disruption

But:
- it is never fully secure
- and never fully comfortable

Which reinforces the city’s core personality:
> You don’t settle. You position.

# 4. Food Economy: Flow, Value, and Leverage

## Core Principle

Food is:
- **ephemeral**
- **unevenly distributed**
- **highly perishable**

So the system optimizes for:
- **speed of discovery**
- **control of access**
- **selective sharing**

No one “owns” food long-term.
But raccoons absolutely control **who gets to eat first, safely, and often**.

---

## The Three Food Classes

Latchkey doesn’t treat all food equally.

---

### 1. **Flash Food**
*High value, zero stability*

Examples:
- pizza slices
- takeout leftovers
- bakery waste
- meat scraps

**Characteristics:**
- discovered suddenly
- consumed quickly
- highly competitive

**Behavior:**
- immediate clustering
- short-lived dominance displays
- rapid depletion

This is the equivalent of a **market spike**.

---

### 2. **Stable Streams**
*Lower intensity, long-term reliable*

Examples:
- consistent residential bins
- predictable restaurant waste
- park trash patterns

**Characteristics:**
- repeatable
- known timing
- moderate value

**Behavior:**
- loosely “claimed” by regulars
- shared through familiarity rather than force
- supports neighborhood stability

This is what actually **sustains population density**.

---

### 3. **Cacheable Food**
*Delayed value*

Examples:
- dense leftovers
- packaged items
- anything that can be carried and hidden

**Characteristics:**
- transportable
- storable (short-term)
- defensible

**Behavior:**
- moved away from source
- hidden in multiple small caches
- sometimes forgotten (which benefits others)

This is the closest thing Latchkey has to **wealth storage**.

---

## Access Control (This is where it gets economic)

There are no laws, but there are **patterns of control**.

### 1. **First Arrival Advantage**
Whoever gets there first:
- eats first
- sets the tone of the interaction

But they rarely hold it alone.

---

### 2. **Familiarity Networks**
Raccoons recognize:
- who shows up regularly
- who escalates conflict
- who tolerates proximity

So access often looks like:
- “we’ve all eaten here before, don’t push it”

This is not cooperation. It’s **mutual risk minimization**.

---

### 3. **Soft Dominance**
Larger or more assertive raccoons:
- don’t guard everything
- but influence spacing and timing

They get:
- first bites
- better positioning

But if they overreach, others:
- wait them out
- reroute entirely

---

## Information as Currency

This is the real engine.

Raccoons don’t “trade food” directly.
They trade **knowledge and tolerance**.

### Examples:

- Knowing a new high-yield dumpster
- Knowing when a household puts bins out early
- Knowing a safe route into a risky zone

How it manifests:
- following specific individuals
- tolerating proximity to “informed” raccoons
- revisiting spots discovered by others

This creates a **reputation layer**:
> Some raccoons are worth being near.

---

## Micro-Logistics (Movement of Food)

Food rarely stays at point of origin.

It moves via:
- short hauls (grab and retreat)
- staged consumption (eat some, move some)
- repeated trips

### Why?
Because safety > volume.

A raccoon would rather:
- make three safe trips
than
- lose everything in one risky attempt

---

## Spatial Economics

Different areas behave differently:

### High-Intensity Zones
- restaurant alleys
- event venues

Result:
- high conflict
- rapid turnover
- opportunistic feeding

---

### Distributed Zones
- residential neighborhoods

Result:
- lower conflict
- predictable sharing patterns
- stronger “familiarity economies”

---

### Dead Zones
- sealed trash systems
- overly sanitized areas

Result:
- bypassed entirely
- used only for transit

---

## Seasonal Shifts

The economy changes dramatically over time:

- **Summer:** abundance, higher tolerance, more juveniles learning
- **Fall:** aggressive caching, competition spikes
- **Winter:** scarcity, reduced movement, reliance on known stable streams

This creates cyclical pressure on:
- housing density
- route usage
- social tolerance

---

## Delightfully Specific Raccoon Choices

- A raccoon will abandon half a bagel if it hears a noise, then return 4 minutes later like nothing happened.
- Fried food is disproportionately valued because of **caloric density vs effort**.
- Individually wrapped snacks are treated like **puzzles worth solving**, not just food.
- Some raccoons specialize in **following humans at a distance**, timing their trash disposal.

---

## System-Level Tradeoff

Latchkey’s food economy is:
- incredibly efficient at **capturing surplus**
- flexible under changing conditions

But:
- it cannot guarantee stability
- it produces constant low-level competition

Which reinforces the city’s deeper truth:
> You are never secure. Only well-positioned.
